Details of how we compute the carbon footprint

Computing the carbon footprint of transportation is tricky to do in sufficient detail. At a high level, the computation involves two components.
  1. The distance travelled
  2. The energy consumed per unit distance
As part of this project, we are greatly improving the accuracy of the first piece. However, for the second piece, we just use the averages for each transportation mode from published data. This means that both individual changes such as switching to more fuel efficient cars, and larger changes such as the electrification of Caltrain, will not affect the calculation.

In the future, we will allow users to specify their vehicle, and also use the fleet-wide carbon emission metrics for transit agencies, which should make this calculation a lot more accurate. This should not be too challenging technically.

For maximum accuracy, we will have to take into account the driving patterns of individual drivers. We will have to experiment with the data that we collect as part of this study, and see if it is possible to infer these patterns from the data that we collect.

In addition, we provide three comparison metrics to frame this number in a larger context.

  1. SB 375 mandates per capita CO2 transportation emission reduction targets for 2020 and 2035. We show the 2035 target.
  2. EO S-3-05 mandates that carbon emissions be reduced to 80% of 1990 levels by 2050. This does not mandate the share of the reduction from the transportation sector. We show a target assuming a proportional reduction, so that transportation emissions are also 80% below 1990 levels by 2050.
  3. Finally, we show the potential carbon footprint achievable if a user changed their commute pattern so that all short trips (< 3 miles) were through non-motorized transport and all long trips (> 3 miles) were through the most efficient motorized transport (train for < 600 miles, air for > 600 miles). This provides users a concrete and achieveable goal that they can work towards.

EO S-3-05 goal

The CARB estimated the 1990 baseline for all emissions as 427 Million Metrics Tons of CO2 emitted (MMTCO2e). The transportation component of that was 38%, or 182.4 MMTCO2e. The passenger vehicle (cars and light-duty truck) component is 70% of that, at 108.5 MMTCO2e.

We can use that to estimate total transportation emissions in 2020 and 2050 assuming that all sectors had to cut their emissions proportionally. Note that the proportional cuts, while reasonable, are NOT mandated in the executive order.

The 1990 population estimate is from the US census bureau. The 2020 and 2050 projections are from the California Department of Finance.

The per capita calculation is per the formula 

per capita kg CO2e/wk = (1,000,000 * 1000 * MMTCO2e) / (population * 52)

Optimal E-Mission

In order to calculate the optimal E-Mission for a particular user, we classified all motorized trips into short (< 3 miles/5km) and long (> 3 miles/5km).
  1. We did this for both transit and private auto trips because the average carbon footprint of a bus, when both peak and off-peak hours are included (267), does not appear to be much lower than that an average private auto (278).
  2. We chose 3 miles (5 km) as the boundary between short and long trips, because we believe that it is a reasonable distance to bicycle. In addition, two 3 mile trips at a 12 mph pace will provide the user with their recommended 30 mins of exercise a day.
  3. The boundary is different for air travel because people don't use planes for 5km trips. Instead, we assume that short trips that are roughly the distance between San Francisco and Los Angeles (~ 600km). Wikipedia claims that the Acela has captured 75% of air/train riders between Washington D.C. and New York City (~ 400km) and 54% of riders between Washington D.C. and Boston (~ 700km), so this seems like a reasonable choice.
